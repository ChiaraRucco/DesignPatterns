PhD Defense Speaker Notes (20 minutes)
Candidate: Chiara Rucco
Title: Pattern-Driven Data Engineering

Usage note:
- These notes are intentionally discursive and detailed.
- Read naturally, not verbatim. Keep eye contact every 2-3 sentences.
- Suggested total time: about 20 minutes.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 1 - Title (0:00-0:50)
Good morning, and thank you for the opportunity to present my doctoral work.
My thesis is titled "Pattern-Driven Data Engineering: Formalizing Systematic Solutions for Cloud-based Data Lakehouses".

The central claim of this work is simple but ambitious: data engineering has reached high technological maturity, but it still lacks the same degree of formalized design knowledge that software engineering achieved through design pattern traditions.

Over the past three years, I developed and validated three pattern artifacts addressing ingestion, transformation timing, and deployment, and I assessed the role of LLMs in implementing such patterns under realistic constraints.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 2 - Research Context and Problem Framing (0:50-2:10)
I begin by framing the problem at a methodological level.
In software engineering, patterns provided a shared vocabulary and a way to reason about recurring solutions, trade-offs, and context-specific applicability.
This facilitated education, communication, and architecture governance.

In data engineering, by contrast, most core architectural decisions are still handled in a local and experience-driven way.
Teams often solve the same classes of problems repeatedly, with limited formal transfer of knowledge between organizations, projects, or even teams in the same organization.

This asymmetry is the starting point of the thesis.
The research question is not only technical, but epistemological: how can we move from tacit operational knowledge to explicit, reusable, and evaluable pattern knowledge in cloud data engineering?

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 3 - Agenda (2:10-2:40)
This presentation follows a strict research logic.
First, I present motivation and methodological approach.
Then I discuss each research question and the corresponding artifact.
After that, I present cross-artifact validation, summarize contributions, discuss limitations and future work, and conclude with the publication trajectory supporting the thesis.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 4 - Motivation: Challenges and Research Questions (2:40-4:00)
The motivation is derived from a systematic literature review and from recurring industrial observations.
I identified four persistent challenge areas.

First, source heterogeneity: ingestion architectures are frequently source-specific and difficult to scale sustainably.
Second, transformation timing: the ETL versus ELT choice is often made heuristically rather than through explicit criteria.
Third, deployment drift: environment-specific configuration handling is a frequent source of fragility and audit issues.
Fourth, the rise of LLMs: strong practical interest, but unclear reliability for production-grade data engineering tasks.

These four challenge clusters are translated into four aligned research questions, each addressed by a dedicated artifact and evaluation strategy.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 5 - Research Approach (4:00-4:50)
Methodologically, I adopted Design Science Research.
This was essential because the thesis aims to produce and validate artifacts, not only describe phenomena.

For artifact specification, I used a POSA-inspired structure including context, problem, forces, solution, and consequences.
This formal template enables comparability and prevents purely narrative pattern descriptions.

Validation combines quantitative and qualitative evidence:
benchmarking where measurable performance is relevant, and expert assessment where transferability, clarity, and usability matter.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 6 - RQ1 Problem: Ingestion (4:50-5:40)
RQ1 investigates how ingestion can be systematized under high source heterogeneity.
The practical issue is that many organizations still encode source-specific logic directly in pipeline code.

As source count and variability increase, this leads to significant maintenance overhead, brittle evolution under schema changes, and reduced reuse.

The literature includes platforms and frameworks, but limited formalization of ingestion as a reusable architectural pattern with explicit strategy-selection logic.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 7 - RQ1 Solution: MIND (5:40-6:50)
The artifact for RQ1 is MIND: Metadata-Driven Ingestion Design.
Its architectural principle is to move variability from code to metadata.

The Unified Metadata Table acts as a declarative control layer that parameterizes ingestion behavior.
This enables controlled reuse across sources and supports strategy selection at runtime.

The key contribution is not simply automation.
It is the separation of concerns between ingestion logic and source-specific characteristics, in a way that remains cloud-agnostic and pattern-formalized.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 8 - RQ1 Results (6:50-7:30)
Empirical and expert results indicate practical relevance.
We observed measurable performance and configuration-effort improvements, and high perceived utility in practitioner evaluation.

Importantly, the pattern was not evaluated as an isolated toy artifact but in realistic platform combinations.
This strengthens the argument for operational transferability.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 9 - RQ2 Problem: Transformation Timing (7:30-8:20)
RQ2 addresses the transformation timing dilemma.
In practice, teams combine early and late transformations, but often without explicit design criteria.

This leads to inconsistent architecture decisions, quality-management ambiguity, and difficulty in explaining why one strategy is preferred in a given context.

Therefore, the problem is not whether ETL or ELT exists; it is the lack of formal decision framing for hybrid transformation pipelines.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 10 - RQ2 Solution: ETLT/ELTL Formalization and ++ Variants (8:20-9:50)
The contribution proceeds in two steps.
First, ETLT and ELTL are formalized as patterns with explicit context, forces, and consequences.
Second, they are extended to ETLT++ and ELTL++ with implementation-level enhancements such as quality gates, contracts, and reject-handling mechanisms.

This makes the design space explicit.
Instead of selecting transformation style by convention, architects can reason from constraints:
latency requirements, data quality tolerance, compliance requirements, and storage/compute trade-offs.

The novelty lies in transforming an implicit practitioner dichotomy into a formal and evaluable pattern framework.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 11 - RQ2 Results (9:50-10:50)
Validation of the enhanced variants shows improvements in throughput and cost dimensions, together with strong expert acceptance.

The three-phase validation design is important here:
technical benchmarking, practitioner evaluation, and known-use evidence.
This triangulation reduces the risk of over-interpreting a single evidence source.

The result is not a universal winner between ETLT++ and ELTL++, but a structured way to justify architectural choice based on context.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 12 - RQ3 Problem: Deployment Drift (10:50-11:40)
RQ3 focuses on deployment reliability across environments.
A recurrent issue is early binding of environment-specific values, which increases drift risk and weakens auditability.

General CI/CD tools are powerful, but they are not inherently data-pattern aware.
They rarely encode data-governance logic, schema-sensitive gates, or provenance-first deployment semantics by default.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 13 - RQ3 Solution: EADF (11:40-12:30)
The artifact is EADF: Environment-Aware Deployment Framework.
Its core principle is late binding with explicit configuration artifacts and promotion governance.

By separating pipeline definition from environment binding, the same logical pipeline can be promoted consistently across environments.
Additional artifact-level controls support quality gates and provenance capture.

This reframes deployment from a script-centric activity to a pattern-governed process.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 14 - RQ3 Results (12:30-13:10)
Observed outcomes include reduced configuration burden, fewer deployment failures, and better traceability.

From an academic perspective, this result is relevant because it links software architecture concerns with governance and reliability concerns in data-intensive systems.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 15 - RQ4 Method: LLM Benchmark Design (13:10-14:10)
RQ4 asks whether LLMs can generate reliable, production-oriented pipeline code.
To answer this rigorously, I designed a controlled benchmark with seven models, three scenarios of increasing complexity, repeated runs, and multiple metrics including correctness and architectural quality.

The benchmark context is intentionally practical: Databricks-oriented pipeline tasks rather than synthetic coding puzzles.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 16 - RQ4 Results: LLMs as Accelerators, Not Replacements (14:10-15:20)
Results show differentiated capability across models and a clear complexity sensitivity.
Several models perform well on simpler tasks, but reliability decreases on multi-source or semantically dense scenarios.

A key finding is that failures are often architectural or semantic, not merely syntactic.
Therefore, LLMs should be positioned as productivity accelerators within a pattern-governed workflow, not as autonomous architecture agents.

This conclusion aligns with the broader thesis logic: formal patterns remain essential as control structures for AI-assisted engineering.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 17 - Cross-Pattern Expert Validation (15:20-16:10)
Across artifacts, expert assessment indicates substantial utility and adoption intent.
This is relevant because the thesis objective is translational: to make academically grounded artifacts useful in real engineering contexts.

The evaluation confirms that practitioners recognize the addressed problems and find the proposed abstractions actionable.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 18 - Contributions (16:10-17:10)
The contributions can be summarized in six points.

First, a formal vocabulary for recurring cloud data engineering problems.
Second, a methodological pathway combining DSR and pattern formalization.
Third, three validated architectural artifacts.
Fourth, evidence of academia-industry bridge through practitioner assessment.
Fifth, a structured lens for evaluating LLM support in data engineering.
Sixth, an open dissemination direction through a pattern catalog.

Taken together, these elements define a coherent doctoral contribution rather than a set of disconnected prototypes.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 19 - Limitations and Future Work (17:10-18:00)
I explicitly acknowledge limitations.
Scope is focused on selected pattern families and cloud contexts.
Expert sample size, while meaningful, is not exhaustive.
LLM findings are time-sensitive due to rapid model evolution.
Cost findings remain context-dependent.

Future work follows directly: expand the catalog to additional domains, integrate pattern constraints into LLM-assisted workflows, and broaden validation across sectors.

------------------------------------------------------------
Slide 20 - Conclusions and Publications (18:00-19:10)
The central conclusion is that formalization improves both scientific rigor and engineering transferability.

This is supported by a publication trajectory from 2024 to 2026 that documents progressive maturation:
ingestion foundations, efficiency extensions, transformation formalization, deployment formalization, and LLM empirical evaluation.

Therefore, the thesis contributes not only new artifacts, but a reproducible way to produce future pattern knowledge in data engineering.
