Modern data engineering faces persistent challenges that require systematic, well-tested solutions.

Design Patterns

Three core patterns addressing fundamental data engineering challenges.

MIND

Metadata-driven INgestion Design

A formalized pattern for orchestrating scalable data ingestion pipelines using a metadata-driven approach. MIND eliminates code duplication by centralizing source configurations, enabling a single generic pipeline to handle diverse sources dynamically.

Cloud-agnostic Full/Incremental/Hybrid Source-agnostic

ETLT/ELTL++

Enhanced Transformation Patterns with Validation

Production-grade transformation patterns with data contracts, quarantine layers, time travel capabilities, and multi-zone architecture. ETLT++ validates before storage; ELTL++ preserves all raw data for reprocessing and compliance.

Data Contracts Time Travel Bronze/Silver/Gold

EADF

Environment-Aware Deployment Framework

Cloud-agnostic CI/CD framework using late binding to resolve environment parameters at deployment time. EADF enables a single artifact to be promoted across dev → staging → prod with zero code changes.

📄 DEFENSE SPEAKER NOTES — 20 min
Candidate: Chiara Rucco — Pattern-Driven Data Engineering Slide 1 — Title (0:00–0:50) Good morning, and thank you for the opportunity to present my doctoral work. My thesis is titled "Pattern-Driven Data Engineering: Formalizing Systematic Solutions for Cloud-based Data Lakehouses". The central claim of this work is simple but ambitious: data engineering has reached high technological maturity, but it still lacks the same degree of formalized design knowledge that software engineering achieved through design pattern traditions. Over the past three years, I developed and validated three pattern artifacts addressing ingestion, transformation timing, and deployment, and I assessed the role of LLMs in implementing such patterns under realistic constraints. Slide 2 — Research Context and Problem Framing (0:50–2:10) I begin by framing the problem at a methodological level. In software engineering, patterns provided a shared vocabulary and a way to reason about recurring solutions, trade-offs, and context-specific applicability. In data engineering, by contrast, most core architectural decisions are still handled in a local and experience-driven way. Teams often solve the same classes of problems repeatedly, with limited formal transfer of knowledge between organizations, projects, or even teams in the same organization. The research question is not only technical, but epistemological: how can we move from tacit operational knowledge to explicit, reusable, and evaluable pattern knowledge in cloud data engineering? Slide 3 — Agenda (2:10–2:40) This presentation follows a strict research logic. First, motivation and methodological approach. Then each research question and the corresponding artifact. After that, cross-artifact validation, contributions, limitations and future work, and the publication trajectory. Slide 4 — Motivation: Challenges and Research Questions (2:40–4:00) The motivation is derived from a systematic literature review and from recurring industrial observations. I identified four persistent challenge areas. 1. Source heterogeneity: ingestion architectures are frequently source-specific and difficult to scale sustainably. 2. Transformation timing: the ETL versus ELT choice is often made heuristically rather than through explicit criteria. 3. Deployment drift: environment-specific configuration handling is a frequent source of fragility and audit issues. 4. The rise of LLMs: strong practical interest, but unclear reliability for production-grade data engineering tasks. Slide 5 — Research Approach (4:00–4:50) Methodologically, I adopted Design Science Research — essential because the thesis aims to produce and validate artifacts, not only describe phenomena. For artifact specification, I used a POSA-inspired structure: context, problem, forces, solution, and consequences. Validation combines quantitative benchmarking and qualitative expert assessment. Slide 6 — RQ1 Problem: Ingestion (4:50–5:40) RQ1 investigates how ingestion can be systematized under high source heterogeneity. The practical issue is that many organizations still encode source-specific logic directly in pipeline code. As source count and variability increase, this leads to significant maintenance overhead, brittle evolution under schema changes, and reduced reuse. Slide 7 — RQ1 Solution: MIND (5:40–6:50) The artifact for RQ1 is MIND: Metadata-Driven Ingestion Design. Its architectural principle is to move variability from code to metadata. The Unified Metadata Table acts as a declarative control layer that parameterizes ingestion behavior. The key contribution is the separation of concerns between ingestion logic and source-specific characteristics, in a way that remains cloud-agnostic and pattern-formalized. Slide 8 — RQ1 Results (6:50–7:30) Empirical and expert results indicate practical relevance. We observed measurable performance and configuration-effort improvements, and high perceived utility in practitioner evaluation. Slide 9 — RQ2 Problem: Transformation Timing (7:30–8:20) RQ2 addresses the transformation timing dilemma. In practice, teams combine early and late transformations, but often without explicit design criteria. This leads to inconsistent architecture decisions, quality-management ambiguity, and difficulty justifying strategy selection. Slide 10 — RQ2 Solution: ETLT/ELTL & ++ Variants (8:20–9:50) First, ETLT and ELTL are formalized as patterns with explicit context, forces, and consequences. Second, they are extended to ETLT++ and ELTL++ with quality gates, contracts, and reject-handling mechanisms. Architects can now reason from constraints: latency requirements, data quality tolerance, compliance requirements, and storage/compute trade-offs. Slide 11 — RQ2 Results (9:50–10:50) Validation shows improvements in throughput and cost dimensions, together with strong expert acceptance. The three-phase validation: technical benchmarking, practitioner evaluation, and known-use evidence — reduces the risk of over-interpreting a single evidence source. Slide 12 — RQ3 Problem: Deployment Drift (10:50–11:40) RQ3 focuses on deployment reliability across environments. A recurrent issue is early binding of environment-specific values, which increases drift risk and weakens auditability. Slide 13 — RQ3 Solution: EADF (11:40–12:30) The artifact is EADF: Environment-Aware Deployment Framework. Its core principle is late binding with explicit configuration artifacts and promotion governance. By separating pipeline definition from environment binding, the same logical pipeline can be promoted consistently across environments. Slide 14 — RQ3 Results (12:30–13:10) Observed outcomes: reduced configuration burden, fewer deployment failures, better traceability. Slide 15 — RQ4 Method: LLM Benchmark Design (13:10–14:10) RQ4 asks whether LLMs can generate reliable, production-oriented pipeline code. I designed a controlled benchmark with seven models, three scenarios of increasing complexity, and multiple metrics including correctness and architectural quality. Slide 16 — RQ4 Results: LLMs as Accelerators, Not Replacements (14:10–15:20) Results show differentiated capability across models and a clear complexity sensitivity. Failures are often architectural or semantic, not merely syntactic. LLMs should be positioned as productivity accelerators within a pattern-governed workflow, not as autonomous architecture agents. Slide 17 — Cross-Pattern Expert Validation (15:20–16:10) Across artifacts, expert assessment indicates substantial utility and adoption intent. Practitioners recognize the addressed problems and find the proposed abstractions actionable. Slide 18 — Contributions (16:10–17:10) 1. A formal vocabulary for recurring cloud data engineering problems. 2. A methodological pathway combining DSR and pattern formalization. 3. Three validated architectural artifacts. 4. Evidence of academia-industry bridge through practitioner assessment. 5. A structured lens for evaluating LLM support in data engineering. 6. An open dissemination direction through a pattern catalog. Slide 19 — Limitations and Future Work (17:10–18:00) Scope is focused on selected pattern families and cloud contexts. Expert sample size, while meaningful, is not exhaustive. LLM findings are time-sensitive due to rapid model evolution. Future work: expand the catalog to additional domains, integrate pattern constraints into LLM-assisted workflows, and broaden validation across sectors. Slide 20 — Conclusions and Publications (18:00–19:10) The central conclusion is that formalization improves both scientific rigor and engineering transferability. This is supported by a publication trajectory from 2024 to 2026: ingestion foundations, efficiency extensions, transformation formalization, deployment formalization, and LLM empirical evaluation. The thesis contributes not only new artifacts, but a reproducible way to produce future pattern knowledge in data engineering.
